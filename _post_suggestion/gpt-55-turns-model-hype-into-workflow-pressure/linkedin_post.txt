The GPT-5.5 model is interesting for a boring reason: it turns model upgrades into workflow upgrades.

Every big release gets framed like an IQ contest. Smarter. faster. more agentic. better at coding. Fine. Useful, even.

But what most engineering teams will actually feel from GPT-5.5 is much less glamorous: whether their coding assistant keeps context across real work, survives multi-step tasks, and produces output that doesn't make code review feel like digital asbestos removal.

That is the shift.

The real evaluation metric for an AI coding model is no longer just benchmark theater. It is workflow reliability. Can it follow the repo? Can it recover from ambiguity? Can it stay efficient when the job stops being "write a function" and becomes "touch six files without wrecking the system"?

That is why the developer productivity conversation is getting more operational. Agentic workflows sound exciting in demos. In real teams, they turn into questions about permissions, reviewability, failure recovery, and cost.

GPT-5.5 might be a stronger model. The more important story is that model releases are starting to behave like workflow decisions.

Are teams choosing a smarter model, or choosing the set of workflow assumptions they are willing to live inside?
