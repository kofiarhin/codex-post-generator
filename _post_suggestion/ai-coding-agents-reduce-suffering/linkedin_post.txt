AI coding agents are getting judged by the wrong standard.

The useful question is not whether they can replace engineers. It is whether they can reduce the dumbest parts of the job: repetitive refactors, context-switching, and the three-hour detour caused by one tiny bug hiding behind a confident stack trace.

That is where the value feels real.

Most experienced developers are not looking for a machine to “do software” for them. We want help with the tedious parts that drain attention before the meaningful work even starts. The first draft is rarely the bottleneck. The bottleneck is the cleanup, the verification, the dependency archaeology, and the slow tax of rebuilding context after every interruption.

AI coding agents look most credible when they reduce that friction.

Not when they cosplay as autonomous engineers.

The teams getting real value from AI developer tools seem to understand that distinction. They use them to speed up code reviews, explore refactor options, surface edge cases, and shorten debugging loops. Judgment, accountability, and maintenance still belong to humans. Thankfully.

That feels like the mature framing for AI coding agents: less engineering theater, less avoidable suffering, more room for actual software engineering.
