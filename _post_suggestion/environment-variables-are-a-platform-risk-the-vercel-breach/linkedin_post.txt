The Vercel breach is a useful reminder that platform environment variables are not just “config.”

A lot of teams still talk about secrets management like the job ends at `.env` files, a vault product, or who can read production. The uglier part lives one layer higher: which OAuth apps and third-party integrations can touch the platform that holds those secrets in the first place.

That is what makes an OAuth supply chain attack so nasty. You can be reasonably disciplined inside your codebase and still hand a trusted app a clean path into deployment metadata, platform environment variables, or automation surfaces you quietly stopped treating as production access.

We keep splitting this into separate conversations: app security, CI/CD security, platform security, marketplace installs, third-party app permissions. Attackers do not care about our org chart.

The practical lesson is boring, which usually means it is real: review OAuth scopes like access grants, not like convenience clicks. Treat developer tooling as part of your attack surface. Rotate secrets assuming integrations leak. And stop pretending “it’s only a platform setting” is a security boundary.

Secret management was probably the easy part. Governing who gets to stand next to the secret store is the part teams keep postponing. How are you handling that tradeoff now?
