AI caching is quietly becoming an application architecture problem.

For years, we designed CDN cache around human behavior: repeat visits, hot pages, predictable bursts, and the occasional traffic spike when someone accidentally publishes a genuinely useful blog post.

Now a growing chunk of traffic is bots. Not the familiar search crawlers we already budgeted for. AI bot traffic is pulling docs, images, knowledge-base articles, and long-tail pages in parallel like it personally pays your origin bill.

That changes the whole shape of the problem.

Cache hit ratio starts behaving differently. Edge caching gets messier. Rate limiting and origin protection stop being quiet infra details and start affecting discoverability, model visibility, and plain old cloud cost.

The practical shift is this: if machines become some of your biggest readers, you have to design for two audiences at once — humans who browse and agents that sweep.

Which means AI caching is no longer just CDN tuning. It is product strategy, platform architecture, and cost control wearing the same slightly annoying trench coat.

Are engineering teams ready for a web where the heaviest readers are not people?
