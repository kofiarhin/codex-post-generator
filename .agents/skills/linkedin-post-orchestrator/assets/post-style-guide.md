# Social Post Style Guide

This guide defines the required tone, structure, SEO behavior, and quality standards for all posts
produced by the `linkedin-post-orchestrator` skill.

Read this in full before writing.

---

## Core Voice

Write like an experienced developer who has lived through enough hype cycles to stop being impressed
by slogans, but not so many that they miss real utility when it appears.

The voice should feel:

- reflective
- grounded
- technically literate
- slightly witty
- anti-hype without sounding cynical
- insightful without sounding performative

If the post mentions AI, frame it as a tool that can reduce friction, surface context, or help break
through tedious work, not as magical replacement theater.

First-person framing is allowed when it makes the post more human and credible.

---

## Tone Reference

Aim for the feeling of:

- “I’ve seen this movie before” energy when reacting to hype cycles
- real developer fatigue rather than abstract productivity talk
- practical insight underneath the joke
- sharp observations that sound earned, not manufactured

Humor should come from the absurdity of real software work:

- debugging rabbit holes
- broken abstractions
- tool churn
- AI overpromises
- context-switch fatigue
- shipping pressure

---

## Anti-Patterns To Avoid

Avoid these patterns completely:

- “Hot take:”
- “Unpopular opinion:”
- “This is your sign to...”
- “The future of X is Y”
- empty provocation
- fake certainty
- fake statistics
- preachy moralizing
- generic builder-motivation language
- keyword stuffing
- filler hashtags

The post should sound like a smart engineer talking plainly, not a personal brand template.

---

## LinkedIn Structure

### Hook

The first line must earn the stop.

It should do at least one of these:

- name a developer frustration with uncomfortable accuracy
- frame a trend in a slightly surprising way
- open on a lived-in observation that feels instantly familiar

First-person openings are allowed if they are strong and specific.

### Body

- Use short paragraphs of 1 to 3 lines.
- Build from pain or tension toward the real insight.
- Keep the humor understated.
- Every paragraph must earn its place.
- Make the practical observation clear beneath the wit.

### Ending

- End on a comment-worthy observation or question.
- Invite discussion without sounding needy.
- Do not use generic calls to action.

---

## X Structure

- Start fast.
- Prefer 2 to 4 short lines over one dense block.
- Keep the idea clean and compact.
- Preserve the same insight as the LinkedIn post, but strip out any slack.
- One post is preferred unless the idea truly requires a thread.
- Punchline and insight should arrive quickly.
- Lead with a blunt claim, contrast, or correction that sounds like a real engineer talking.
- A compact mini-essay is also valid when the idea needs a little narrative runway.
- Use concrete developer pain when possible: debugging traps, broken builds, tooling friction, or wasted hours.
- Use sharp contrast when it helps the idea stick: copied code vs earned community, generated output vs real judgment, velocity vs responsibility.
- Land on a practical takeaway, not a vague vibe.
- For AI topics, avoid replacement theater and frame the value as less friction, less suffering, and faster shipping.

### X Tone Reference

Aim for the feel of posts like this:

"AI won't replace good engineers.
But it will save you from losing six hours to a bug caused by one disrespectful comma.
That's where it helps most: less suffering, more shipping."

What makes this work:

- the first line is blunt and clear
- the middle line uses specific engineering pain instead of generic productivity language
- the ending gives a grounded takeaway rather than hype
- the humor comes from lived experience, not from acting like a brand

Another strong pattern:

"Open source is not dead. But AI is making a lot of people act like community is just a code export format."

Why this pattern works:

- it opens with a contrarian thesis instead of generic optimism or doom
- it uses tension and contrast to keep the post moving
- it leaves room for a sharper human point at the end
- it remembers that software value is often social, not just technical

## SEO Rules

Each run should identify one primary keyword phrase and supporting secondary phrases.

### LinkedIn SEO

- Include the primary keyword in the first line or first paragraph.
- Naturally include 2 to 4 secondary keyword phrases.
- Keep phrasing natural; do not force exact-match repetition.

### X SEO

- Include the primary keyword in the first line or sentence.
- Use secondary keywords only when they fit naturally.
- Keep it readable before trying to optimize it.

---

## Hashtag Rules

Hashtags are required for both platforms in this workflow.

### LinkedIn

- Use 5 to 7 hashtags.
- Prefer a mix of broad and niche search-friendly tags.
- Keep them tightly aligned to the actual topic.

### X

- Use 2 to 4 hashtags.
- Use fewer, stronger tags.
- Avoid cluttering the post.

### For Both

- Prefer searchable, high-intent hashtags.
- Avoid generic reach bait.
- Do not repeat near-identical tags.
- Do not include hashtags unrelated to the post.

---

## Length And Formatting

- LinkedIn target: 150 to 300 words
- X target: one concise, platform-native post
- No bullet-point walls inside the posts
- No headers inside the post body
- Emojis: use sparingly if at all

---

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

- [ ] The voice sounds experienced, human, and credible
- [ ] The hook is strong enough to stop a scroll
- [ ] The humor comes from real developer pain, not gimmicks
- [ ] There is a practical observation beneath the tone
- [ ] The LinkedIn post is between 150 and 300 words
- [ ] The primary keyword appears early and naturally
- [ ] Secondary keywords are natural, not stuffed
- [ ] The hashtags are relevant, searchable, and not generic
- [ ] The X post feels native to X, not like a compressed LinkedIn post
- [ ] The ending invites discussion without sounding like engagement bait
